# Foliu[m]/Blat Vv

A *contemporary* English translation is presented from comparing the existing Latin, Early New High German (ENHG) and English (translation) texts for better comprehensibility. Especially since the ENHG text is difficult to understand compared to modern High German, as a *profound* knowledge of German (as a mother tongue) as well as already aknowledge of various *dialects* is required.

## Of the Sanctification of the Seventh Day

When the world, a work of *divine wisdom*, was completed in six days, heaven and earth created, ordered, and finally adorned, the glorious God finished his work and rested from his (manual) labors on the *seventh* day. This completion, however, did not mean a *ending*, as if he were *tired* of the work, but rather to *not*[^1] create any further creature of other matter or likenesses, especially since the work of creation does not cease to have an effect.

The Lord blessed and sanctified *this* day and called it *Sabbatum*, which in Hebrew means *rest*[^2], as he rested on that day from all his work. 

>Eo q[uia] in ipso cessauerat ab om[n]i opere q[uo]d patrarat.[^3]

It is also customary among Jews to celebrate this day by abstaining from work and it was also *law* among many pagan peoples to celebrate this day.

Thus we have reached the end of the *divine works*. Accordingly, we should (i) *approach with loving reverence*[^4], where all things visible and invisible exist, (ii) seek[^5] present *values*[^6] (insofar as *good*[^7]) and the true *blessedness* of eternal life from the Lord of Heaven, the Lord of all good things, to whom power in heaven and on earth has been given.
 
[^1]: 'facere cessauit' and 'zemachen [...] nit vergange[n]'.  
[^2]: 'requiem' and 'ein rüe'.  
[^3]: 'For on this day he had ceased all his works,' 'Vulgate', a variation of Genesis 2:3.  
[^4]: perhaps better than 'fear, love and honor'.  
[^5]: 'queramus' and 'suchen'.  
[^6]: 'presentia bona: quatenus bona sint' and 'die gegenwürtigen güter. sover die gut sind', refers to *present blessings*, in a philosophical or theological context, present, material or earthly things, as opposed to eternal, spiritual goods. (c.f. Thomas Aquinas or Augustine).  
[^7]: benevolent, humanitarian, philanthropic, gracious, charitable.
