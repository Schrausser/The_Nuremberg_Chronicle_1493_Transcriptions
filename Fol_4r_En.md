# Foliu[m]/Blat IIIIr

A *contemporary* English translation is presented from comparing the existing Latin, Early New High German (ENHG) and English (translation) texts for better comprehensibility. Especially since the ENHG text is difficult to understand compared to modern High German, as a *profound* knowledge of German (as a mother tongue) as well as already aknowledge of various *dialects* is required.


## On the Work of the Fourth Day

>**Q**Uarto die dixit deus.
Fia[n]t luminaria in firmame[n]to celi: [et] diuidant diem et nocte[m].  
Et sint in signa [et] t[em]p[er]a: [et] dies: [et] a[n]nos:  vt lucea[n]t in firmame[n]to celi: [et] illumine[n]t terra[m].  
Et factu[m] e[st] ita.  
Fecitq[ue] deus duos luminaria magna: luminare maius: vt p[re]esset diei: [et] luminare min[us]: vt p[er]esset nocti [et] stellas: vt duiderent luce[m] [et] tenebras.

**Moses** first mentions the celestial bodies[^1] that God placed in the firmament so that they might shine in the heaven[^2] and illuminate the *earth*, namely the sun, moon, and stars[^3] with which the upper[^4] part of the *world* itself is adorned, just as the *earth* is adorned with the metals, plants and living beings[^5] that grow in it. After speaking about the nature of the firmament, it remained for him to speak about the workings of the stars[^6] and their function, to explain for what purpose they were created and for what task God assigned them.

The two modes of action of celestial bodies[^7], *movement* and *illumination*, are universally recognizable, resulting in a *twofold* movement (I): One is (i) the entire world[^8], in which heaven and ether rotate in a perfect circle through the entire space of the universe[^9] in 24 hours. The other is the actual, multifaceted, and varied movement of (ii) the celestial bodies[^10], with the main movement of the sun, which passes through all the signs of the zodiac within twelve months. This creates the day, from which the daily cycles form the year. The remaining movements of the stars occur at different intervals. Rightly, *Moses* aptly and succinctly reminded us that the stars were placed in the firmament for days, years, and seasons.

He also explicitly pointed out the other effect of the stars, which is *illumination*[^11] (II), when he said that they were arranged to shine in the heaven[^12] and to light up the earth. Thus, the bodies of the moon, the sun and the stars[^13] are distributed[^14] for this purpose[^15]. Although the sun only *apparently* rises alone during the day (**Cicero** probably wants it to appear that only *one* sun is recognized)[^16], since it *appears* alone through the veiled stars[^17], *it* (the sun) is nevertheless the true and perfect light that illuminates everything with the greatest *warmth* and the brightest *brilliance*[^18]. For although countless stars seem to twinkle and shine, those whose light is not full and clear neither provide warmth nor dispel the darkness through their multitude.  Thus, *two* main elements can be found, (i) *warmth* and (ii) *'glory'*[^19], which possess a diverse and at the same time cooperating power, wonderfully created by God for the preservation and creation of all things.

However, this would require addressing *very profound* questions, each of which would require its own book: (i) In what way are these stars located in the firmament, whether as its *nobler* parts or as animals[^20] in their spheres, like fish in water or animals on earth.[^21] This point also required a discussion with (ii) astrologers[^22] regarding divination by the stars and the prediction of future events, which science confirmed by stating that *Moses* said the stars were placed by God as signs. Furthermore, (iii) the nature of the stars, their motion, their orbits, the *spots*[^23] on the moon, and the entire science of the stars (astronomy *and* astrology)[^24] must be addressed.

Truly,

>qua[m]q[uoniam] sint pulcra [et] digna cognitu.[^25]

Maybe we will hear *this* **Horace**, but here was not the right place.
